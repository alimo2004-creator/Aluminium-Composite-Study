# 🔩 Processing for Performance: Enhancing Aluminum's Specific Flexural Strength

> **Result:** 📈 150.88% increase in flexural strength · 597% increase in specific flexural strength

---

## 📌 Project Overview

Raw aluminum sheets in their as-supplied form rarely meet real engineering performance requirements. This project set out to **significantly enhance the specific flexural strength** of a provided aluminum sheet (Grade 5454) through structural and material modifications — without exceeding a final thickness of **15mm**.

The final product is a **sandwich composite panel** featuring:
- An **aluminum honeycomb core** for structural rigidity
- **Fiberglass face sheets** bonded top and bottom
- **Foam-filled cavities** to reduce density
- **Epoxy bonding** throughout the assembly

---

## 🎯 Objective

Maximize the **specific flexural strength** (flexural strength / density) of the aluminum sheet using:

- Cold working / rolling
- Heat treatment
- Composite material construction
- Innovative structural design (honeycomb sandwich panel)

---

## 🧪 Material

The supplied aluminum sheet was chemically analyzed and identified as **Grade 5454** (Al-Mg alloy, ~95.58% Al, 2.87% Mg).

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Grade | 5454 (Al-Mg) |
| Key alloying element | Mg: 2.871% |
| Heat-treatable? | ❌ No — not suitable for precipitation hardening |
| Microstructure | Yellow matrix with dark spots (observed under optical microscope) |

> Since Grade 5454 is **not heat-treatable**, the team pivoted to composite and structural solutions.

---

## ⚙️ Methodology

### Design Approach
After confirming the alloy was not heat-treatable and that cold rolling equipment was unavailable, the team designed a **honeycomb sandwich composite** as follows:

| Step | Process |
|------|---------|
| 1 | Cut aluminum strips and form hexagonal honeycomb cells manually |
| 2 | Straighten cell walls using cold working (manual hammer) |
| 3 | Assemble honeycomb core and secure with crocodile clips |
| 4 | Bond core with epoxy steel (instant adhesive) |
| 5 | Fill cavities with foam to further reduce density |
| 6 | Cut face sheets (240×30×1 mm) into halves |
| 7 | Bond fiberglass sheets (×4) to top and bottom surfaces |
| 8 | Roughen all bonding surfaces for better epoxy adhesion |
| 9 | Compress the full assembly and cure |
| 10 | Trim excess epoxy using sandpaper and iron file |

---

## 📐 Testing

3-point bending tests were performed on an **LR 300K Universal Testing Machine** following **ASTM C393**.

### Flexural Strength Formula:

$$\sigma = \frac{3PL}{2bh^2}$$

Where:
- **P** = Load at midspan (N)
- **L** = Span length (mm)
- **b** = Width (mm)
- **h** = Thickness (mm)

---

## 📊 Results

| Property | Raw Aluminum | Composite Panel | Change |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|--------|
| Density (g/cm³) | 2.66 | 1.13 | ↓ 57.5% lighter |
| Flexural load (N) | 30 | 2,366 | ↑ 78.9× |
| Flexural strength (MPa) | 28.8 | 85.4 | ↑ **196.5%** |
| Specific flexural strength | 10.83 | 75.58 | ↑ **597.7%** |
| Ductility | High | Low (trade-off) | — |

> **Key finding:** The composite panel achieved a **150.88% increase in flexural strength** and a **597% improvement in specific flexural strength** — making it highly suitable for high-load, weight-sensitive structural applications.

## 💡 Key Takeaways

- Honeycomb sandwich panels are an extremely effective structural solution when heat treatment is not applicable
- Fiberglass face sheets significantly increase load-bearing capacity with minimal weight penalty
- Foam filling reduces density further, boosting the strength-to-weight ratio
- Surface roughening before epoxy bonding is critical to joint strength
